Alchemite Analytics is a machine learning tool developed to optimise R&D in materials, chemicals, and life sciences. This case study highlights my contribution to the end-to-end process of developing the new "Projects" feature in Alchemite Analytics.

the challenge.

Our users needed a way to manage their Models into Projects.

research.

collecting research.

So, how could we design a better way of managing your Models in Alchemite?

Working closely with our science consultants and chatting with users, I gathered plenty of qualitative and quantitative data to support our problem statement. It was a hands-on experience that really helped us understand the problem-space.

data.

What were our customers saying?

We discovered that our users' projects were inherently iterative, often involving multiple versions of the same model with minor tweaks. Without an efficient way to organise their models, they were forced to sift through pages containing potentially hundreds of models—a significant pain point, especially for our older customers who had accumulated models over the years."

design.

Collaborating with the Product Trio, we developed user-centered solutions and formulated hypotheses to test.

How did we develop designs?

To kick off the project, we gathered the Product Manager, two Engineers, and a Science rep for an ideation session. We went over the project scope and summed up our research findings to make sure everyone was on the same page about the problem we're tackling.

After that, we threw out some 'How might we' questions, borrowed from Design Sprint methods, and got the team brainstorming ideas.

design constraints.

Within the trio, we were able to identify the business constraints and user constraints that would impact the decision-making process.

design workshops.

Working with the Product Trio and Engineers we were able to create user centred solutions.

wireframes.

I lead workshops with the trio to collaboratively solve the problem and create wireframes that would solve the user’s problem. After iterating, we were able to agree on a couple of designs to test with users.

user testing.

usability tests.

Each iteration of our designs when through a usability testing round where we would prototype the design and live test it with users. This allowed us to measure user behaviours against our business goals and inform the next design decisions.

feasibility meeting.

Once deciding on the final design, it was time to discuss the ideas with the engineering team and product manager. At this stage, we would discuss which mini features could and couldn’t be included in the project. This was also the time to discuss functionality in more depth and agree on high-level ways to solve these problems.

design system.

The new feature required a couple of new components, such as a new type of modal which would allow the user to share the project with groups. Whilst designing the interface it was important to follow our design system to ensure the new feature was consistent with the old interface.

2nd most used feature.

The new feature quickly rose to the second most utilised, with usage steadily increasing by 10% each week over the first month.
